## Synthesis

The current evidence from eight systematic reviews and meta-analyses, encompassing over 300,000 participants across hundreds of randomized controlled trials, supports the conclusion that GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) reduce the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) — a composite endpoint that includes non-fatal stroke — in patients with type 2 diabetes. The class-level MACE reduction is consistently estimated at 14-18% (HR 0.82-0.86) compared to placebo across multiple independent analyses (Hasebe et al., [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.70458); Ahmed et al., [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12872-025-05455-4); Yeo et al., [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.70298)). This benefit appears robust across racial and ethnic groups, with Asian populations showing a significantly greater relative risk reduction (HR 0.73) compared to White populations (HR 0.86), an important equity consideration for diverse patient populations (Hasebe et al., [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.70458)).

When stroke is analyzed as an individual endpoint rather than as part of the MACE composite, the evidence is more nuanced. The network meta-analysis by Shokravi et al. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12933-026-03113-3)) found that tirzepatide, a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, significantly reduced non-fatal stroke by 21% (HR 0.79, 95% CI 0.64-0.97) compared to placebo. Drug-specific analyses from the umbrella review by Yeo et al. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/dom.70298)) suggest dulaglutide may have a particularly favorable stroke profile (eOR 0.78, 0.68-0.90). However, the semaglutide-specific meta-analysis by Tan et al. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcvm.2025.1731127)) did not find a statistically significant reduction in non-fatal stroke as an isolated endpoint (despite significant MACE reduction, HR 0.83), and the preclinical-to-clinical translational review by Michaelsen et al. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1161/STROKEAHA.125.053075)) highlights that promising neuroprotective mechanisms observed in animal stroke models have not yet translated into demonstrable clinical stroke benefits in nondiabetic patients. Cerebrovascular protection may also extend beyond acute stroke events: Stefanou et al. ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jdiacomp.2026.109271)) found a numerically lower incidence of vascular dementia with GLP-1 RA therapy (RR 0.50), though this did not reach statistical significance in 61,610 participants across 7 RCTs.

In comparative terms, GLP-1 RAs appear to provide greater atherosclerotic event reduction (including stroke components of MACE) than SGLT2 inhibitors (HR 0.82 vs. 0.89 for MACE), while SGLT2 inhibitors show stronger benefits for heart failure and renal outcomes (Ahmed et al., [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12872-025-05455-4)). Bariatric surgery may provide even greater MACE reduction (HR 0.65) compared to GLP-1 RA therapy in patients with obesity, though this evidence comes from observational studies with inherent confounding (Cordova & Malaga, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.soard.2025.11.024)). These comparisons underscore that GLP-1 RAs, SGLT2 inhibitors, and surgical interventions are complementary rather than interchangeable strategies for cardiovascular risk reduction in type 2 diabetes.

## Clinical Bottom Line (for the M2 student)

**Answer to your question**: Yes, GLP-1 receptor agonists reduce the risk of stroke in patients with type 2 diabetes, but primarily as part of their broader cardiovascular benefit (MACE reduction of ~15-20%). When stroke is isolated as an individual endpoint, the evidence is statistically significant for some agents (tirzepatide, dulaglutide) but not all (semaglutide as monotherapy). The effect is consistent across racial groups and is complementary to SGLT2 inhibitor therapy.

**For your pharmacology exam**: Key pharmacology points include the mechanism of action (incretin mimetic → enhanced insulin secretion, suppressed glucagon, delayed gastric emptying, appetite suppression), the cardiovascular benefit appears independent of glucose lowering (likely through anti-inflammatory and anti-atherogenic mechanisms), and the class effect on MACE is a defining feature that differentiates GLP-1 RAs from older diabetes therapies like sulfonylureas or thiazolidinediones.
